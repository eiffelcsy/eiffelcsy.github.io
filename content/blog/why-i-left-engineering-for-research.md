I spent three years as a software engineer before starting to do research. The transition was harder than I expected in some ways and easier in others. Here's what I learned.

## The thing I got wrong about research

I thought research was about having ideas. It's mostly about having questions. The ideas come later, and they're usually less original than you hoped. What matters is whether you're asking questions that have non-obvious answers and that someone cares about.

It took me about a year to stop generating ideas and start sitting with questions long enough for them to become real.

## What engineering prepared me for

More than I expected. The habit of actually building things — of not just theorizing but running experiments and looking at what comes out — turns out to be useful in research. A lot of ML research papers describe systems that the authors haven't really stress-tested. Having a background in shipping software made me uncomfortable with that.

Also: debugging. Most of a researcher's time, in my experience, is spent figuring out why the experiment didn't work the way the theory said it should. This is debugging. Engineers are good at this.

## What I wish I'd known

The feedback loops are much longer. In engineering, you ship something and find out relatively quickly whether it was a good idea. In research, you can spend six months on a direction before you know whether it's going anywhere.

The way I've learned to cope with this is to structure projects so there are smaller experiments along the way that give you partial signal. Not all projects admit this, but most do if you're careful about how you design them.

## Would I do it again?

Yes, without hesitation. The questions I'm working on now are the ones I've been most curious about for most of my life. That's hard to put a price on.
